I. Introduction

In many under-resourced legal systems‚Äîoften in so-called developing countries‚Äîjustice is slow, fragmented, and often undermined by informal practices. One curious yet widespread procedural custom involves the refusal of courts to award attorneys‚Äô fees for the litigation required to determine the amount of attorneys‚Äô fees in a previous case.

To simplify: Imagine a lawyer wins a lawsuit (Case A) and then initiates a new proceeding (Case B) to contest the fee amount. Even if the lawyer wins Case B, courts frequently refuse to award fees for the work done in Case B, arguing that one cannot ‚Äúget fees for getting fees.‚Äù This doctrine has no real statutory basis and is seldom justified in depth. The core judicial rationale in many jurisdictions can be reduced to the idea that Case B‚Äîa dispute over the proper fee‚Äîis not an independent legal controversy but a procedural side-effect of Case A. Therefore, it generates no entitlement to further fees.

This logic fails in two key ways:

  • It erases actual legal work: If the lawyer had to file pleadings, respond to opposition, attend hearings, and perhaps appeal a ruling in Case B, then real legal labor occurred. Why should that go uncompensated?

  • It violates fairness norms: If a lawyer must go to court to enforce or adjust their own remuneration, the legal system cannot ask them to perform that work for free‚Äîespecially when the court requires a full procedural process to be followed.

1. Justice Is Being ‚ÄúSubsidized‚Äù by Those Who Deliver It

When courts refuse to award fees for fee-related litigation, they shift the cost of the legal system‚Äôs own procedures onto the shoulders of the least powerful actors‚Äîindividual lawyers, especially those outside elite firms or without retainer contracts.

2. It Discourages the Enforcement of Fair Compensation

Ironically, a system that discourages lawyers from litigating fee disputes undermines its own accountability. If no one fights low or unfair fee rulings because doing so is unpaid, bad practices become entrenched.

If a legal system signals that lawyers who fight for their pay do so at their own cost, it sends a clear message: legal labor is not worth defending. That‚Äôs a dangerous precedent for the long-term integrity of any legal profession.

  • Why do judges get paid to decide how much lawyers should be paid, but lawyers get nothing for making their case?
  • Is justice really ‚Äúaccessible‚Äù if those delivering it can‚Äôt afford to work?

VI. Conclusion: A False Economy

The idea that refusing to compensate legal labor ‚Äúmakes justice more affordable‚Äù is a false economy. The cost is real‚Äîit‚Äôs simply borne by those least equipped to carry it. And when lawyers are disincentivized from enforcing their own rights, the rule of law suffers.
